The honest answer to "how much house can I afford?" is usually less than what a lender will approve you for. Banks size your loan against your gross income; your actual life runs on take-home pay, after retirement contributions, groceries, childcare, and the occasional broken water heater. This guide walks through the math lenders use, then the math *you* should use.

## Start With the 28/36 Rule

The **28/36 rule** is the fastest sanity check in home buying. It sets two ceilings:

- **28% front-end ratio** — Your monthly housing payment should stay at or below 28% of your **gross monthly income** (income before taxes).
- **36% back-end ratio** — All your monthly debt payments combined — housing *plus* car loans, student loans, minimum credit card payments — should stay at or below 36% of gross monthly income.

Some loan programs stretch these limits (FHA and well-qualified conventional borrowers sometimes go to 43%–50% back-end). Treat those higher numbers as the edge of what's *allowed*, not a target.

### What counts as the "housing payment"

This is where buyers get tripped up. The housing payment isn't just principal and interest. Lenders measure **PITI**:

- **P**rincipal
- **I**nterest
- **T**axes (property taxes)
- **I**nsurance (homeowners insurance)

Add **PMI** (private mortgage insurance) if your down payment is under 20%, plus any **HOA dues**. A mortgage payment quote that only shows principal and interest can understate your real cost by hundreds of dollars a month.

## Debt-to-Income: The Number That Actually Decides

Your **debt-to-income ratio (DTI)** is the back-end ratio above, and it's the lever lenders care about most. Lower DTI means more borrowing room and better odds of approval.

The fastest way to afford more house often isn't earning more — it's clearing a car payment or knocking out a credit card balance. Every $400 monthly payment you eliminate frees up that same $400 for housing under the 36% cap. If you're carrying balances, run the numbers on the [debt payoff calculator](/calculators/debt-payoff) before you shop; the payoff can shift your price range more than a rate change.

## A Worked Example

Meet a household earning **$96,000 a year**, or **$8,000 gross per month**. They have a **$350 car payment** and **$150 in student loans** — $500 in existing debt.

**Step 1 — Front-end cap (28%):**
$8,000 × 0.28 = **$2,240** for total housing (PITI).

**Step 2 — Back-end cap (36%):**
$8,000 × 0.36 = $2,880 for all debt. Subtract their $500 in existing payments: $2,880 − $500 = **$2,380** left for housing.

**Step 3 — Take the lower number.**
The front-end cap ($2,240) is lower, so that's their working housing budget.

**Step 4 — Back out the home price.**
PITI isn't all loan. Roughly a quarter of it here goes to taxes, insurance, and PMI. If about **$1,700** of the $2,240 covers principal and interest, that supports a loan in the neighborhood of **$270,000–$290,000**, depending on the rate and term. Add their down payment, and that's the price range.

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Gross monthly income | $8,000 |
| Front-end cap (28%) | $2,240 |
| Back-end cap (36%) | $2,880 |
| Existing monthly debt | $500 |
| Room for housing (back-end) | $2,380 |
| **Working housing budget (PITI)** | **$2,240** |
| Est. principal &amp; interest | ~$1,700 |
| Est. supported loan | ~$270k–$290k |

Because rates and tax rates vary by location, plug your own figures into the [mortgage calculator](/calculators/mortgage) to turn a monthly payment into a price.

## Don't Forget the Cash You Need Up Front

Affordability isn't only a monthly question. You need cash to close — and cash left over after closing.

**Down payment.** Conventional loans can go as low as 3% down; FHA as low as 3.5%. Putting 20% down lets you skip PMI, but draining every dollar to hit 20% is usually a mistake.

**Closing costs.** Budget roughly **2%–5% of the purchase price** for lender fees, title, appraisal, and prepaid taxes and insurance. On a $300,000 home that's $6,000–$15,000 on top of the down payment.

**Emergency fund after closing.** This is the rule first-time buyers skip and later regret. Keep **three to six months of expenses** in the bank *after* you've paid the down payment and closing costs. Homes generate surprise expenses — a roof, an HVAC unit, a sump pump — and you don't want a maintenance bill turning into credit card debt.

## Lender Math vs. Real-Life Math

The 28/36 rule runs on gross income, but you spend net income. A few adjustments keep you honest:

- **Budget from take-home pay.** If you max out a 401(k) and pay for health insurance pretax, your gross can be 25%–30% higher than what actually hits your account.
- **Account for goals the bank ignores.** Childcare, college savings, travel, and retirement don't show up in DTI — but they compete for the same dollars.
- **Stress-test the payment.** Ask whether the PITI still feels comfortable if your income dropped 10%, or if property taxes rose.

A simple way to pressure-test the monthly number is to slot it into a spending plan. The [50/30/20 budget calculator](/calculators/budget) caps "needs" — including housing — at 50% of take-home pay, which is often a tighter, more realistic ceiling than 28% of gross.

## Quick Affordability Checklist

- Housing PITI ≤ 28% of gross monthly income
- Total debt ≤ 36% of gross monthly income
- Down payment saved (and you know your PMI situation)
- Closing costs (2%–5%) set aside separately
- 3–6 month emergency fund survives after closing
- Payment still comfortable on take-home pay
