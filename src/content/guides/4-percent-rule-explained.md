If you've ever wondered how big your nest egg needs to be before you can stop working, you've probably bumped into the **4% rule**. It's the single most-cited rule of thumb in retirement planning — short enough to fit on an index card, useful enough to anchor a decades-long decision. It's also widely misunderstood. Here's exactly what it says, the math behind it, and the specific situations where it stops being a safe bet.

## What the 4% rule actually says

The rule has two parts, and people usually only remember the first one.

1. In your **first year** of retirement, withdraw 4% of your portfolio's value.
2. Every year after that, adjust the **dollar amount** for inflation — not 4% of the new balance, but last year's withdrawal plus the rise in the cost of living.

So if you retire with $1,000,000, you take out $40,000 in year one. If inflation runs 3% that year, you take out $41,200 the next year — regardless of whether your portfolio went up or down. You're giving yourself a steady, inflation-protected "paycheck" rather than reacting to the market each year.

The 4% figure comes from research on historical U.S. markets. Financial planner **William Bengen** published the original analysis in 1994, testing withdrawal rates against every rolling 30-year period in the historical record. The later **Trinity Study** (1998) reached a similar conclusion using a different method. Both found that a portfolio of stocks and bonds had a high historical success rate at a 4% starting withdrawal over 30 years — meaning the money lasted the full period in nearly all the historical windows tested.

## The flip side: the 25x rule

Here's the part that makes the rule genuinely useful for planning. If you can safely withdraw 4% per year, then your target portfolio is simply the inverse:

> 1 ÷ 0.04 = **25**

In other words, you need roughly **25 times your annual expenses** saved before the 4% rule applies. This is the same idea stated backwards, and it turns a vague goal ("save enough to retire") into a concrete number.

### Worked example

Say you expect to spend **$50,000 per year** in retirement.

| Step | Calculation | Result |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Annual spending | — | $50,000 |
| Target portfolio (25x) | $50,000 × 25 | **$1,250,000** |
| Year-one withdrawal (4%) | $1,250,000 × 0.04 | $50,000 |
| Year-two withdrawal (3% inflation) | $50,000 × 1.03 | $51,500 |

Note one thing this math ignores: any **other income**. Social Security, a pension, or part-time work all reduce the spending your portfolio has to cover, which lowers your target. If Social Security will cover $20,000 of that $50,000, your portfolio only needs to produce $30,000 — a $750,000 target instead of $1.25M. ## When the 4% rule breaks

This is where the rule earns its caveats. It was never a law of nature — it's a backtest of a specific market, time horizon, and portfolio. Change any of those, and the safety margin shrinks.

### Sequence-of-returns risk

This is the big one. Two retirees can earn the **same average return** over 30 years and end up in completely different places — because *when* the bad years hit matters enormously.

If a market crash arrives in your **first few years** of retirement, you're selling assets at depressed prices to fund withdrawals, locking in losses while the portfolio is largest. The account may never recover. The exact same crash arriving in year 20 is far less dangerous, because earlier growth gave you a cushion. The 4% rule's historical success rate already bakes in some bad sequences, but a genuinely terrible early stretch can still drain a portfolio faster than the rule assumes.

### Retirements longer than 30 years

Bengen's research targeted a **30-year** retirement — sensible for someone retiring at 65. But if you retire at 50, or pursue FIRE (financial independence, retire early), you might need the money to last **40 or 50 years**. Over longer horizons the same 4% rate carries a meaningfully higher chance of running out, simply because there are more years for things to go wrong. Many early retirees use a lower rate to compensate.

### Low-return environments

The original studies drew on a century of U.S. history that included strong long-run stock returns and periods of higher bond yields. If future returns are lower than the historical average — whether from elevated valuations, low yields, or slow growth — the 4% rule's cushion thins. Past performance, as always, is not a guarantee.

### Rising and lumpy healthcare costs

The rule assumes spending rises smoothly with general inflation. Real retirement spending isn't that tidy. **Healthcare costs** have historically climbed faster than overall inflation, and big-ticket events — a major procedure, long-term care — arrive as lumps, not gentle annual increases. A flat inflation adjustment can understate what you'll actually need.

## Alternatives people use instead

The 4% rule isn't the only option, and most thoughtful retirees adapt it:

- **A lower starting rate (3.5% or 3%).** The simplest hedge against long retirements and low returns. A 3.5% rate raises your target to roughly **28.5x** expenses, but buys a wider margin of safety.
- **Guardrails (the Guyton-Klinger approach).** Set upper and lower "guardrails." If the market does well and your withdrawal rate drifts low, you give yourself a raise; if it drops and your rate climbs too high, you trim spending. This responds to reality instead of ignoring it.
- **Flexible / variable withdrawals.** Spend a fixed percentage of the *current* balance each year. Your income fluctuates, but you can never fully run out — the withdrawal shrinks with the portfolio.
- **A cash buffer.** Hold one to three years of expenses in cash so you can pause selling stocks during a downturn, directly softening sequence-of-returns risk.

## The honest bottom line

The 4% rule is a **planning guideline, not a guarantee.** Its real value is the 25x framing: it converts "retirement" into a target number you can actually aim at. Treat 4% as a starting estimate, stay willing to adjust spending when markets misbehave, and account for your own time horizon and income sources.
