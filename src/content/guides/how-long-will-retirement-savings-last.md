The honest answer is: it depends, but you can get a surprisingly useful estimate in about five minutes. How long your money lasts comes down to three things you can actually measure — how much you've saved, how much you withdraw each year, and what your investments earn while you're spending them down. This guide walks through each, shows roughly how withdrawal rate maps to longevity, and covers the risks (and fixes) that determine whether your savings outlive you or the other way around.

## Start With the 4% Rule as a Backward Lens

Most people know the **4% rule** as a spending guideline: withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one, adjust that dollar amount for inflation each year after, and historically a balanced stock/bond portfolio had a strong chance of lasting **at least 30 years**.

Flip it around and it becomes a longevity gauge. If 4% is roughly a 30-year withdrawal rate, then:

- Withdrawing **more** than 4% shortens how long the money lasts.
- Withdrawing **less** stretches it, potentially indefinitely.

A quick way to ballpark your starting number: take your portfolio and multiply by 0.04. A $750,000 portfolio supports about **$30,000 per year** (before Social Security). If that's not enough, you either need a bigger portfolio or a higher — and riskier — withdrawal rate. For the full mechanics and the research behind it, see [The 4% Rule, Explained](/guides/4-percent-rule-explained).

## The Three Levers That Control Longevity

Everything about drawdown reduces to three variables:

1. **Portfolio size** — your starting balance. Bigger cushion, more years.
2. **Withdrawal amount** — what you pull out annually. The single lever you control most directly day to day.
3. **Investment return during retirement** — what the remaining balance earns. You don't control this, but your asset allocation shapes the range of outcomes.

The trap is thinking of these as independent. They interact. A portfolio earning 6% can sustain a much higher withdrawal than one earning 2% — and returns in retirement are never a smooth average, which is where the real risk hides.

## How Withdrawal Rate Maps to Years (Roughly)

The table below is directional, not a promise. It assumes withdrawals grow with inflation and shows the general relationship between your withdrawal rate and how long a diversified portfolio tends to last. Actual results swing widely with market returns and the order in which they arrive.

| Initial withdrawal rate | On $1,000,000 (year 1) | Approximate longevity* |
|---|---|---|
| 3% | ~$30,000 | 40+ years / often never depleted |
| 4% | ~$40,000 | ~30 years with reasonable confidence |
| 5% | ~$50,000 | ~20–25 years, return-dependent |
| 6% | ~$60,000 | ~15–18 years, meaningful shortfall risk |

\*Inflation-adjusted withdrawals from a balanced portfolio. These are illustrative ranges, not guarantees — a poor first decade can cut years off the high end, and a strong one can extend the low end.

The takeaway: each percentage point of withdrawal rate has an outsized effect. Going from 4% to 5% isn't a 25% bigger income — it's roughly a decade off your timeline in tougher markets.

## Sequence-of-Returns Risk: Why the First Years Matter Most

Two retirees can experience the *same average return* over 30 years and end up in wildly different places — one comfortable, one broke — based purely on **when** the bad years hit.

Here's why: when you're withdrawing, a market crash early in retirement forces you to sell more shares at low prices to fund the same spending. Those shares are gone; they can't recover when the market rebounds. This is **sequence-of-returns risk**, and it's most dangerous in roughly the **first 5–10 years** of retirement.

A 30% drop in year two of retirement does far more damage than the identical drop in year 25, even though the average return looks the same on paper. This is why blindly trusting a "my portfolio averages 7%" assumption is dangerous in the drawdown phase.

## Inflation Quietly Erodes Every Withdrawal

A fixed $40,000 withdrawal feels stable, but at **3% inflation** its buying power roughly halves over about 24 years. To maintain your lifestyle, your withdrawals have to *grow* — which is exactly what the 4% rule assumes and exactly what makes high withdrawal rates so fragile. Plan in **real (inflation-adjusted) dollars**, not today's nominal numbers, or you'll overestimate how long your money truly lasts.

## Social Security and Pensions Shrink the Job Your Portfolio Has to Do

Your portfolio rarely has to fund your entire retirement. **Social Security**, and a **pension** if you have one, cover a baseline — and that dramatically reduces the longevity math.

Say you need $60,000 a year and Social Security provides $28,000. Your portfolio only has to generate **$32,000**, not $60,000. On an $800,000 portfolio, that's a 4% withdrawal instead of 7.5% — the difference between "very sustainable" and "likely to run out." Every guaranteed dollar of income is a dollar your portfolio doesn't have to risk.

## Strategies to Make It Last Longer

You're not locked into a single fixed withdrawal. The most resilient plans stay flexible:

- **Use flexible withdrawals.** Spend a bit less in down years (skip the inflation raise, trim discretionary spending) and you can support a higher baseline rate. Even small "guardrail" cuts after bad years meaningfully extend longevity.
- **Hold a cash buffer.** Keep **1–2 years** of expenses in cash or short-term bonds so you can pause portfolio withdrawals during a crash instead of selling stocks at a loss. This directly blunts sequence-of-returns risk.
- **Add part-time income early.** Earning even $15,000–$20,000 in the first few high-risk years lets your portfolio recover untouched. The early years are where flexibility pays off most.
- **Delay Social Security.** Waiting from 62 toward 70 increases your benefit by roughly **8% per year** of delay — an inflation-adjusted, lifelong raise that's hard to beat and shrinks the load on your portfolio.
- **Right-size your allocation.** Too conservative and inflation wins; too aggressive and a crash hurts. A balanced mix keeps growth in play without exposing you to catastrophic early drawdowns.

## Putting It Together

How long your savings last is a moving target you can steer. Pin down your three levers, build in a buffer for the early years, lean on guaranteed income, and stay willing to adjust. The combination of a **modest withdrawal rate**, a **cash cushion**, and **flexibility** is what turns "I hope this lasts" into "this will very likely last."
