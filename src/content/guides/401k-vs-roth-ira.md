If your employer offers a 401(k) match, contribute enough to capture all of it before you do anything else with your retirement money. That's free money with an instant return no investment can reliably beat. After that, the **401(k) vs Roth IRA** question comes down to one thing: do you want your tax break now or in retirement?

This guide breaks down how each account works, when each wins, and the exact order to fund them.

## The core difference: tax now vs. tax later

Every retirement account gives you a tax break. The only question is *when* you get it.

- A **traditional 401(k)** uses **pre-tax** money. Contributions lower your taxable income today, the money grows tax-deferred, and you pay ordinary income tax when you withdraw in retirement. Tax break now, tax bill later.
- A **Roth IRA** uses **after-tax** money. You get no deduction today, but the money grows tax-free and qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. Tax bill now, tax break later.

The simple rule: if you expect to be in a **higher tax bracket** in retirement than you are today, Roth tends to win. If you expect a **lower bracket** later, traditional pre-tax tends to win. Younger workers and anyone early in their earning years often lean Roth, because they're likely paying lower rates now than they will later.

Note: many employers now also offer a **Roth 401(k)**, which combines the high 401(k) contribution limit with Roth tax treatment. If that's available, it changes the math below — but the principles are the same.

## 401(k) vs Roth IRA at a glance

| Dimension | 401(k) | Roth IRA |
|---|---|---|
| Who offers it | Your employer | You open it yourself at any broker |
| Contribution source | Payroll deduction (pre-tax, or Roth if offered) | After-tax dollars you transfer in |
| Tax treatment | Deduction now, taxed at withdrawal (traditional) | No deduction now, tax-free at withdrawal |
| Contribution limit | Much higher | Much lower (check the current-year IRS limit) |
| Income limits to contribute | None | Yes — phases out at higher incomes |
| Employer match | Often yes | No |
| Investment choices | Limited menu chosen by your plan | Nearly unlimited (any stock, ETF, or fund) |
| Required withdrawals | Yes for traditional (RMDs in retirement) | None during your lifetime |

## The employer match always comes first

A typical match might be "50% of contributions up to 6% of pay." Put in 6%, and your employer adds 3% on top — an immediate 50% return before the market does anything.

Skipping the match to fund a Roth IRA first is almost always a mistake. **Capture the full match, then decide where the next dollar goes.**

## Income limits on the Roth IRA

The Roth IRA has a catch the 401(k) doesn't: **income limits**. Once your income climbs past a certain threshold, your ability to contribute directly phases out and eventually disappears. Those thresholds change every year, so always check the **current-year IRS limits** before assuming you qualify.

If you earn too much, look into a **backdoor Roth IRA** — a legal workaround where you contribute to a traditional IRA and convert it to Roth. It has tax wrinkles, so research it carefully or ask a tax pro.

The 401(k), by contrast, has no income limit. High earners can always contribute.

## Early-withdrawal basics

Retirement accounts are built to stay locked until age 59½. Pull money out early and you generally owe income tax plus a 10% penalty.

One meaningful Roth IRA advantage: because you already paid tax on your **contributions**, you can withdraw the amount you contributed (not the earnings) at any time, tax- and penalty-free. That makes a Roth IRA a more flexible backstop than a 401(k) — though raiding retirement savings should still be a last resort.

401(k)s sometimes allow loans or hardship withdrawals, but rules vary by plan and the costs add up fast.

## The priority order: where each dollar should go

For most people, fund accounts in this order:

1. **401(k) up to the full employer match.** Free money. Non-negotiable.
2. **Pay off high-interest debt.** Credit cards charging 20%+ beat any expected investment return. Knock these out before investing more.
3. **Max out a Roth IRA (if eligible).** Tax-free growth, flexible withdrawals, and a broad menu of low-cost investments. Check the current-year limit.
4. **Go back and max out the 401(k).** Once the Roth is full, return to the 401(k) and push toward the annual maximum.
5. **Taxable brokerage account.** For anything beyond those limits.

This order captures free money first, eliminates guaranteed-loss debt second, then stacks tax-advantaged growth from most-flexible to least.

## The bottom line

There's rarely a pure "401(k) *or* Roth IRA" choice — most people benefit from using both. Take the match, clear high-interest debt, fund a Roth IRA while you qualify, then max the 401(k). Match the tax treatment to where you expect your bracket to land, and let time do the heavy lifting.
