You've got cash sitting in a checking account earning next to nothing, and you know it should be working harder. The two safe, boring, FDIC-insured options most people land on are a **high-yield savings account (HYSA)** and a **certificate of deposit (CD)**. They look similar on the surface, but the difference comes down to one word: access. Picking the right one is mostly about answering "when do I need this money?"

## What a High-Yield Savings Account Is

A **high-yield savings account** is a regular savings account that pays a much higher interest rate than the national-average brick-and-mortar bank account, usually because it's offered online with lower overhead.

Its defining traits:

- **Variable rate.** The APY can change at any time, and the bank doesn't need your permission. When the Federal Reserve moves rates, HYSA yields tend to follow within weeks.
- **Fully liquid.** You can move money in and out, typically with no penalty. (Some banks still cap certain withdrawal types per month, so check the fine print.)
- **FDIC-insured** up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category. Your principal is never at market risk.

The trade-off you're accepting: you give up rate certainty in exchange for being able to grab your money whenever you want.

## What a Certificate of Deposit Is

A **certificate of deposit** is a deposit you agree to leave untouched for a fixed **term** (common terms run from 3 months to 5 years) in return for a **fixed rate** that's locked the day you open it.

Its defining traits:

- **Fixed rate.** Whatever APY you lock in is yours for the entire term, no matter what happens to rates afterward.
- **Limited access.** Pull the money out before the term ends and you'll usually pay an **early-withdrawal penalty**, often a set number of months of interest (e.g., 3 months' interest on a 1-year CD, more on longer terms). A steep penalty can eat into your principal if you withdraw early enough.
- **FDIC-insured** under the same $250,000 limits as savings.

The trade-off here is the mirror image: you give up easy access in exchange for a rate you can count on.

## HYSA vs CD at a Glance

| Feature | High-Yield Savings (HYSA) | Certificate of Deposit (CD) |
|---|---|---|
| **Rate type** | Variable — can rise or fall anytime | Fixed — locked for the full term |
| **Access to funds** | Anytime, fully liquid | Locked until maturity |
| **Early-withdrawal penalty** | None (occasional withdrawal limits) | Yes — typically several months' interest |
| **Best for** | Emergency funds, near-term or undated goals | Money needed at a known future date |
| **FDIC insured** | Yes, up to $250k per depositor/bank/category | Yes, up to $250k per depositor/bank/category |

## The Core Trade-Off: Liquidity vs a Locked Rate

Everything above boils down to two questions you should ask before parking a dollar anywhere.

**Might I need this money soon, or on no fixed schedule?** Then liquidity wins, and that points to a HYSA. The variable rate is a fair price to pay for being able to access the cash the day you need it.

**Do I know I won't touch this money until a specific date?** Then locking a rate with a CD can pay off, because you're rewarded for committing and you're protected if rates fall before your date arrives.

## When Each One Wins

### Your emergency fund → HYSA

An emergency fund only does its job if you can reach it the moment an emergency hits. A car repair or a job loss won't wait for a CD to mature. Keep your emergency fund in a HYSA, where it stays liquid and still earns a competitive rate.

Not sure how much that should be? See [How Big Should Your Emergency Fund Be?](/guides/emergency-fund-guide) before you decide how much to set aside.

### Money for a known future date → CD

Saving for a down payment 18 months out, a tax bill, or a wedding next spring? You know the date, and you won't touch the money before then. A CD whose term lines up with that date lets you **lock today's rate** so a future rate cut can't shrink your return. It also adds a small psychological barrier against spending the money early.

## CD Laddering: A Way to Get Both

You don't have to choose all-or-nothing. A **CD ladder** splits your money across several CDs with staggered maturity dates.

A simple example with $10,000 across five rungs:

- $2,000 in a 1-year CD
- $2,000 in a 2-year CD
- $2,000 in a 3-year CD
- $2,000 in a 4-year CD
- $2,000 in a 5-year CD

Each year a CD matures, freeing up cash. You either spend it or roll it into a new 5-year CD at whatever rate is then available. The payoff:

- **Regular access.** Something comes due every year, so you're never fully locked up.
- **Rate averaging.** You capture longer-term rates without betting everything on a single moment in time.
- **Reinvestment flexibility.** If rates have climbed, each maturing rung rolls into a higher rate.

## What Happens If Rates Rise or Fall

This is where the two products behave very differently, and it's the part people most often get wrong.

**If rates rise:**

- *HYSA:* You benefit automatically. Your APY drifts up to follow the market with no action needed.
- *CD:* You're stuck at your old, lower rate until the term ends. Locking a long CD right before a rate climb is the main risk of CDs.

**If rates fall:**

- *HYSA:* Your APY drops too, and your earnings shrink.
- *CD:* You're protected. Your locked rate keeps paying the old, higher yield for the rest of the term. This is exactly when a CD shines.

The honest summary: **HYSAs are better when rates are climbing or uncertain; CDs are better when you expect rates to fall and you can commit the cash.** Since none of us can reliably predict the Fed, a HYSA for liquid money plus a CD ladder for dated goals hedges both directions.

## A Quick Decision Checklist

- **Emergency fund or undated savings?** HYSA.
- **Need it within a year and the date's fuzzy?** HYSA.
- **Specific date, won't touch it, want rate certainty?** CD (or a ladder).
- **Big sum, want access and a locked rate?** CD ladder.
- **Think rates are about to fall?** Lean CD. **About to rise?** Lean HYSA.

Most people end up using both: a HYSA as the always-available base layer, and one or more CDs for money with a deadline.
