The single biggest difference between a Roth IRA and a Traditional IRA is **timing: when the IRS takes its cut.** A Traditional IRA generally lets you deduct contributions now and pay income tax later, when you pull the money out in retirement. A Roth IRA flips that — you contribute money you've already paid tax on, and qualified withdrawals later come out completely tax-free, growth included.

Everything else (income limits, required withdrawals, early-access rules) flows from that one distinction. Here's how to figure out which side of the tax timeline works in your favor.

## The Core Difference: Pay Tax Now or Pay Tax Later

Both accounts are individual retirement arrangements you open yourself, separate from any workplace plan. Both let your investments grow without yearly taxes on dividends or capital gains. The fork is purely about *when* tax applies:

- **Traditional IRA** — Contributions may be **tax-deductible** in the year you make them, lowering your taxable income now. Your money grows tax-deferred, and every dollar you withdraw in retirement is taxed as ordinary income.
- **Roth IRA** — Contributions are made with **after-tax dollars**, so there's no deduction today. In exchange, qualified withdrawals in retirement — original contributions *and* all the growth — are tax-free.

Think of it as paying tax on the seed (Roth) versus paying tax on the harvest (Traditional).

## The Decision Rule: Your Tax Bracket Now vs. in Retirement

The cleanest way to choose is to compare your **marginal tax rate today** with the rate you expect in retirement.

- **If you expect a higher tax rate in retirement, lean Roth.** You'd rather pay tax now at today's lower rate and take tax-free withdrawals later. This often fits younger workers, people early in their careers, and anyone who expects rising income.
- **If you expect a lower tax rate in retirement, lean Traditional.** Taking the deduction now at a high rate and paying tax later at a lower rate works in your favor. This often fits peak-earning years.
- **If you genuinely can't predict, splitting contributions** across both gives you **tax diversification** — a mix of taxable and tax-free buckets to draw from, which adds flexibility when tax law and your income are both uncertain.

No one knows future tax rates with certainty, so treat this as an educated bet, not a guarantee. A [retirement calculator](/calculators/retirement) can help you model both scenarios with your actual numbers.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA |
|---|---|---|
| **Tax treatment of contributions** | Potentially deductible now | After-tax (no deduction) |
| **Tax on qualified withdrawals** | Taxed as ordinary income | Tax-free |
| **Growth** | Tax-deferred | Tax-free if qualified |
| **Income limits to contribute** | None to contribute; income affects deductibility | Yes — high earners may be limited or phased out |
| **Deduction phase-outs** | Yes, if you/spouse have a workplace plan | N/A |
| **Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)** | Yes, starting at the IRS age | None during the owner's lifetime |
| **Early access to contributions** | Generally penalized before 59½ | Contributions withdrawable anytime, tax- and penalty-free |
| **Best suited for** | Those expecting a lower future tax rate | Those expecting a higher future tax rate |

## Income and Deduction Rules to Know

This is where the two accounts get fussy, and where the details change every year. **Always verify the current-year figures on IRS.gov before you contribute** — the numbers below are concepts, not amounts.

### Traditional IRA: Deductibility Can Phase Out

Anyone with earned income can *contribute* to a Traditional IRA, but the **deduction** isn't always full. Whether you can deduct your contribution depends on:

- Your **modified adjusted gross income (MAGI)**, and
- Whether you (or your spouse) are **covered by a workplace retirement plan** like a 401(k).

If you're covered by a plan at work and your income is above a certain threshold, your deduction shrinks and can disappear entirely. You can still contribute — it just becomes a *nondeductible* contribution, which changes the math considerably. The exact phase-out ranges are set by the IRS and adjusted annually.

### Roth IRA: Income Limits Cap Eligibility

Roth IRAs have a different gate: **direct contributions phase out at higher incomes.** Earn above the IRS limit for your filing status and you may be allowed only a reduced contribution, or none at all. These thresholds also change yearly, so check the current figures rather than relying on last year's.

## RMDs: A Quiet but Major Advantage for Roth

Traditional IRAs come with **Required Minimum Distributions** — once you hit the IRS-specified age, you *must* start withdrawing a minimum amount each year and pay tax on it, whether you need the money or not.

**Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the original owner's lifetime.** You can let the balance keep compounding tax-free for as long as you like, which makes the Roth a powerful tool for both flexibility and passing wealth to heirs. ## Early-Withdrawal Flexibility

Life happens before 59½, and here the Roth is notably more forgiving.

- **Roth IRA:** Because you already paid tax on contributions, you can withdraw your **original contributions** (not the earnings) at any time, for any reason, with no taxes or penalties. Earnings withdrawn early are a different story and can trigger taxes and a penalty.
- **Traditional IRA:** Early withdrawals are generally hit with both **income tax and a 10% penalty**, with limited exceptions.

A Roth is not a substitute for an emergency fund, but that accessible-contribution rule makes it a gentler choice if your savings are tight.

## Who Should Pick Which?

**Lean Roth IRA if you:**
- Are early in your career or expect higher future income
- Want tax-free income and no RMDs in retirement
- Value the ability to tap contributions in a pinch

**Lean Traditional IRA if you:**
- Are in a high tax bracket now and expect a lower one later
- Want the upfront deduction to reduce this year's tax bill
- Don't qualify for a Roth due to income limits (though a nondeductible Traditional has trade-offs worth researching)
