Paying off your mortgage early can save you tens of thousands of dollars in interest and hand you a debt-free home years ahead of schedule. But it isn't automatically the right move, and the answer depends heavily on your interest rate, your other debts, and what else you could do with the money. This guide walks through every method that actually works, then makes the honest case for *not* prepaying.

## Why Extra Principal Early Saves the Most

Mortgages use **amortization**, which front-loads your interest. In the early years, most of your monthly payment goes toward interest and only a sliver toward the balance. As the balance shrinks, the interest portion shrinks too, so later payments chip away at principal much faster.

That structure is exactly why **extra principal payments made early** are so powerful. A dollar you put toward principal in year 2 erases years of future interest on that dollar; the same dollar in year 25 saves almost nothing because there's barely any interest left to avoid.

One rule matters above all: **every extra dollar must be applied to principal**, not prepaid toward next month's bill. Many servicers default extra money to "the next payment." Write "apply to principal" on the check or use the dedicated principal-only field in your online portal, then confirm it landed correctly on your next statement.

## The Methods That Work

### Extra Principal Every Month

Add a fixed amount to each payment, earmarked for principal. On a typical 30-year loan, an extra $200–$300 a month can cut several years and a five-figure sum in interest off the loan. The beauty is flexibility: you can pause it any month without penalty.

### One Extra Payment a Year / Biweekly

Pay **half your monthly payment every two weeks** and, because there are 52 weeks, you make 26 half-payments, equal to **13 full monthly payments a year** instead of 12. That one "extra" payment typically shaves roughly 4–6 years off a 30-year mortgage, depending on your rate.

You don't need a paid biweekly program to get this benefit. Just divide one monthly payment by 12 and add that amount to each check yourself. See [Biweekly Mortgage Payments](/guides/biweekly-mortgage-payments) for the full mechanics and the fees to avoid.

### Lump Sums

Tax refunds, bonuses, or an inheritance can go straight to principal. Because the effect compounds over the remaining loan, a single well-timed lump sum early in the loan punches far above its size. There's no need to do it monthly — one-time hits count.

### Recasting

A **mortgage recast** (or re-amortization) lets you apply a large lump sum to principal, after which the lender recalculates your monthly payment over the *original* payoff date. You keep your existing rate and term, but your monthly payment drops. It usually costs a small fee (often $150–$500) and doesn't shorten the term — it lowers the payment instead. Good for freeing up monthly cash flow; less aggressive than just throwing the lump sum at principal and keeping payments high.

### Refinancing to a Shorter Term

Swapping a 30-year loan for a **15-year mortgage** typically gets you a lower rate and forces faster payoff. The trade-off is a higher required monthly payment, and you'll pay **closing costs** (roughly 2–5% of the loan). This only makes sense if rates have fallen or you can comfortably absorb the bigger payment, since unlike voluntary extra payments, it's now mandatory.

## Payoff Methods Compared

| Method | How it works | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Extra principal monthly** | Add a set amount to each payment | Flexible, easy to start/stop, big savings | Requires discipline; verify it hits principal |
| **Biweekly / 13th payment** | 26 half-payments = one extra payment/year | "Painless" extra payment, ~4–6 yrs saved | Avoid paid setup fees; DIY instead |
| **Lump sum** | One-time principal payment | High impact early, no commitment | Ties up cash you can't easily get back |
| **Recast** | Lump sum + re-amortized lower payment | Keeps low rate, lowers monthly payment | Small fee; doesn't shorten the term |
| **Refinance to 15-yr** | New shorter-term loan | Lower rate, forced payoff | Closing costs; higher required payment |

## The Honest Counterpoint: Should You Even Do This?

Prepaying a mortgage gives you a **guaranteed, risk-free return equal to your interest rate**. That's genuinely attractive when your rate is high. But it's a weaker deal at the low rates many homeowners locked in, and there are several things that should come *first*.

### Do These Before Prepaying a Dollar

- **Build an emergency fund** of 3–6 months of expenses. Money sunk into your house is illiquid — you can't pull a principal payment back out to cover a job loss without selling or borrowing.
- **Clear high-interest debt.** Paying off a 22% credit card beats prepaying a 6% mortgage every time.
- **Capture your full 401(k) match.** A 50–100% employer match is an immediate return no mortgage payoff can touch.

### The Opportunity-Cost Question

If your mortgage rate is **low (say 3–4%)**, the math often favors investing instead. Historically, a diversified stock-index portfolio has returned more than that over long periods, though with real risk and no guarantee. The wider the gap between your rate and your expected investment return, the stronger the case for investing.

At a **6–7%+ rate**, prepaying looks much better, because beating that return after taxes and risk is harder. There's no universal answer; it hinges on your rate, your timeline, and your tolerance for risk. We break the decision down further in [Should You Pay Off Debt or Invest First?](/guides/pay-off-debt-or-invest).

### The Mortgage Interest Deduction Nuance

People often overstate this. The mortgage interest deduction **only helps if you itemize**, and since the standard deduction was roughly doubled, most households now take the standard deduction and get **no marginal tax benefit** from mortgage interest. Even if you do itemize, the deduction only offsets a fraction of the interest — it never makes paying interest "free." Don't keep a mortgage purely for the write-off.

### The Intangibles

Money isn't only math. For many people, **owning the home outright** brings real peace of mind and lower fixed costs in retirement that no spreadsheet fully captures. If a paid-off house helps you sleep at night, that's a legitimate reason — just go in knowing the trade-off.
