If you invest **$100,000** today and leave it untouched for 30 years, a **7% annual return** turns it into roughly **$761,000** — more than seven times your starting amount, without adding a single dollar along the way. That's the power of **compound interest** doing the work while you do nothing.

The catch: 7% is a reasonable historical average, not a promise. Your actual result depends on what rate you earn and what inflation does to the dollar. Below is the math at three common rates, plus the part most people skip — what that future pile of money will actually buy.

## The short answer

A one-time **$100,000 lump sum**, compounded annually with **no additional contributions**, grows like this:

| Annual return | Value after 30 years | Growth multiple |
|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| 4% (conservative) | ~$324,300 | 3.2x |
| 7% (historical stock-market average) | ~$761,200 | 7.6x |
| 10% (optimistic) | ~$1,744,900 | 17.4x |

The spread is enormous: a 3-percentage-point bump from 7% to 10% more than *doubles* the ending balance. Over long horizons, small differences in return — or in fees, which quietly subtract from your return — compound into life-changing gaps.

## Why the growth looks so dramatic

Compounding means you earn returns **on your past returns**, not just on your original $100,000. In year one at 7%, you make $7,000. By year 20, your balance is already around $387,000, so that same 7% is now generating roughly $27,000 a year. The growth curve bends upward and steepens toward the end — which is exactly why **time in the market** matters more than almost anything else.

A quick way to sanity-check this is the **Rule of 72**: divide 72 by your return rate to estimate how many years it takes your money to double. At 7%, that's about 10.3 years, so $100,000 doubles roughly three times over 30 years (to ~$200k, ~$400k, ~$800k) — landing right near our $761,000 figure.

## The part that gets ignored: inflation

That **$761,000** sounds spectacular, and it is — but it will be spent in *future* dollars, which buy less than today's.

At roughly **3% inflation**, prices about **2.4x** over 30 years. So your $761,000 would have the buying power of approximately **$315,000 in today's dollars** (these are approximate — actual inflation varies year to year). Still a strong outcome: your **real**, inflation-adjusted wealth roughly tripled. But it reframes the goal. You didn't turn $100k into "nearly a million" in any meaningful sense — you roughly tripled what it can actually buy.

This is why financial planners focus on **real returns** (return minus inflation) rather than the headline number. A 7% nominal return is closer to a 4% real return once inflation takes its cut — which, not coincidentally, is why the 4% row in the table above is a decent stand-in for "what this is worth in today's money."

## What this means for your plan

A few practical takeaways:

- **Don't touch it.** The biggest gains happen in the final decade because the base is so large. Cashing out early forfeits the steepest part of the curve.
- **Watch fees.** A 1% annual fee doesn't cost you 1% — over 30 years it can quietly erase six figures, because that money never gets to compound.
- **The rate assumption is everything.** "7%" reflects long-run **U.S. stock market** averages with dividends reinvested. A portfolio heavy in bonds or cash will track closer to the 4% line. There's no guaranteed rate, and returns won't arrive in a smooth, straight line.
- **Think in real dollars.** When you picture that future balance, mentally discount it for inflation so you're planning around what it will actually buy.
