The most common mistake in the **rent vs buy** debate is comparing your rent check to a mortgage payment and declaring the cheaper one the winner. That comparison is broken. A mortgage payment builds equity; rent doesn't. But owning also drags along a pile of costs that renters never touch, and the down payment you sink into a house is money that could have been growing somewhere else. To answer "which is actually cheaper," you have to compare *total cost of occupancy* on both sides, and that math swings hard depending on how long you stay.

## The upfront cost of buying

Renting asks for a security deposit and maybe first and last month's rent. Buying asks for a lot more before you get the keys:

- **Down payment.** Anywhere from 3% (some conventional and FHA loans) to 20% to avoid private mortgage insurance. On a $400,000 home, that's $12,000 to $80,000.
- **Closing costs.** Typically **2% to 5% of the loan amount** — lender fees, title insurance, appraisal, escrow setup, prepaid taxes and insurance. On that same home, plan for roughly $8,000 to $20,000.
- **Moving and setup.** Movers, immediate repairs, appliances, and the furniture needed to fill more square footage than you rented.

This cash is largely **unrecoverable on day one**. If you sell soon after buying, closing costs on both ends (you'll pay another 5% to 6% in agent commissions and fees when selling) can wipe out years of appreciation. That's the heart of why timing matters so much.

Before you shop, it's worth pinning down two numbers: how much home fits your budget and how much cash you'll need saved. See [How Much House Can You Afford?](/guides/how-much-house-can-you-afford) and [How Much Should You Save for a House Down Payment?](/guides/how-much-to-save-for-a-house).

## The ongoing costs owners forget

A mortgage payment is only part of what an owner spends each year. The recurring extras are easy to underestimate:

- **Property tax.** Varies widely by location, but **roughly 1% of home value per year** is a reasonable national ballpark. On a $400,000 home, that's about $4,000 annually — and it tends to rise over time.
- **Homeowners insurance.** Required by your lender and meaningfully pricier than renters insurance.
- **Maintenance and repairs.** A common rule of thumb is **about 1% of the home's value per year**, averaged over the long run. Roofs, HVAC, water heaters, and plumbing don't fail on a schedule, but they do fail.
- **HOA dues.** If you buy a condo or a home in a managed community, monthly HOA fees can run anywhere from modest to several hundred dollars — and they don't build equity either.

Renters, by contrast, pay rent and renters insurance, and their landlord eats the property tax, the broken furnace, and the new roof.

## The opportunity cost of your down payment

Here's the cost almost everyone leaves out. That $80,000 down payment isn't just spent — it's *tied up*. Had you invested it instead, it could have earned a return. Economists call this the **opportunity cost of capital**, and over a long horizon it's not small.

This is also why an all-cash purchase isn't automatically "free." Whether you borrow or pay cash, your money is locked into the house instead of compounding elsewhere. Home equity is real wealth, but it's illiquid and historically appreciates more slowly than a diversified stock portfolio.

## The 5% rule: a cleaner way to compare

A useful shortcut for the **annual unrecoverable cost of owning** is the **5% rule**. The idea: each year, owning a home costs you roughly 5% of its value in money you'll never get back, split into three buckets:

- **~1% property tax** — gone each year.
- **~1% maintenance** — gone each year.
- **~3% cost of capital** — the opportunity cost of your down payment plus the mortgage interest you pay (which, especially in early years, is mostly interest, not principal).

Add them up: about **5% of the home's value per year** in unrecoverable cost. On a $400,000 home, that's roughly **$20,000 a year, or about $1,667 a month**.

The punchline: **if you can rent a comparable home for less than $1,667 a month, renting is the cheaper option** — and you can invest the difference. If renting that same home costs more, buying likely comes out ahead. The 5% figure is a starting estimate, not gospel; adjust the cost-of-capital piece up or down based on your interest rate and expected investment returns, and adjust property tax to match your actual area.

## The breakeven horizon: why 5 years matters

The reason "rent vs buy" has no universal answer is that buying front-loads its costs. You pay the big closing costs up front and the big selling costs at the end, while equity and appreciation accumulate slowly in between.

That creates a **breakeven horizon** — the number of years you'd need to own before buying beats renting. For most buyers in most markets, that's commonly **around 5 years or more**. Sell before then and the transaction costs alone often mean renting would have been cheaper.

What pushes the breakeven shorter or longer:

- **Shorter (buying wins sooner):** low purchase price relative to rents, strong appreciation, low interest rate, low closing costs, a long planned stay.
- **Longer (renting wins longer):** high home prices relative to rents, flat or falling values, high rate, steep HOA or taxes, or any real chance you'll move soon.

## When renting is the smarter money move

Buying is not automatically the responsible choice. Renting can be the better financial decision when:

- **You might move within a few years** — a job change, relationship shift, or simple uncertainty makes the breakeven math risky.
- **Buying eats your emergency fund.** Stretching to the down payment and then having no cash buffer for a $9,000 HVAC replacement is how owners end up in debt.
- **Rents are cheap relative to home prices** in your area, so the 5% rule favors renting and investing the gap.
- **You value flexibility and zero maintenance** — and you actually invest the money you'd otherwise tie up, rather than spending it.

Renting is not "throwing money away." You're buying flexibility, predictable costs, and a landlord who fixes the roof. Owning isn't a scam either — it's forced savings plus a hedge against rising rents. Neither is universally cheaper.

## Costs renters pay vs costs owners pay

| Cost | Renters | Owners |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly housing payment | Rent | Mortgage principal + interest |
| Upfront cash | Security deposit (~1–2 months) | Down payment + closing costs |
| Insurance | Renters insurance (cheap) | Homeowners insurance (higher) |
| Property tax | No (landlord pays) | Yes (~1%/yr) |
| Maintenance & repairs | No (landlord pays) | Yes (~1%/yr) |
| HOA dues | Usually no | Yes, if applicable |
| Opportunity cost of capital | Minimal | Down payment tied up |
| Builds equity | No | Yes (slowly at first) |
| Selling costs | None | ~5–6% commissions + fees |

## Run your own numbers

The honest answer to "rent vs buy" depends on your price, your rate, your local taxes, and how long you'll stay. Plug in real figures and see where your breakeven lands.
