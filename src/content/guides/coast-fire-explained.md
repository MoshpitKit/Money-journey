There's a specific moment in a long savings journey when the heavy lifting is over. You've invested enough that, even if you never contribute another dollar, compound growth alone will carry your portfolio to a full retirement nest egg by the time you're 60 or 65. That moment is called **Coast FIRE**, and it's one of the most useful milestones to aim for because it arrives years — often decades — before you can actually quit working.

This guide explains exactly what Coast FIRE is, how it differs from the other FIRE flavors, and how to run the numbers yourself. You can also skip straight to the [Coast FIRE calculator](/calculators/coast-fire) and plug in your own figures.

## What Coast FIRE actually means

**Coast FIRE** is the point at which your invested assets are large enough that, *without any further contributions*, they will grow to your full retirement number by traditional retirement age.

Once you hit your coast number, your retirement is effectively on autopilot. You still need an income — but only enough to cover your **current living expenses**. You no longer have to save a cent for retirement, because the money already invested is doing that job for you over the coming decades.

That's the psychological catch that makes Coast FIRE so appealing. It's not "stop working forever." It's "stop working *so hard for the future*."

## How it compares to the other FIRE types

FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) comes in several varieties. They share the same engine — invest, let it compound, live off a safe withdrawal rate — but differ in how much you need and when you stop.

- **Full FIRE** — You have roughly **25x your annual expenses** invested (the flip side of a 4% withdrawal rate). At this point you can stop working entirely.
- **Lean FIRE** — Full FIRE built on a deliberately frugal budget, so your number is smaller.
- **Fat FIRE** — The opposite: a generous budget, so your number is much larger.
- **Barista FIRE** — You've saved enough that a modest part-time job (plus its benefits) covers the gap until full retirement.
- **Coast FIRE** — You've saved enough that you *never need to invest again*, but you still work to cover today's bills.

The key distinction: Full and Lean/Fat FIRE are about *not needing a job at all*. Coast and Barista FIRE are about *needing far less from a job*.

## The math behind your coast number

There are two steps. First, find your full retirement target. Then discount it back to today.

**Step 1 — Your FIRE number.** This is the portfolio that can fund your retirement at a chosen withdrawal rate:

```
FIRE number = annual retirement expenses / withdrawal rate
```

At the standard 4% rate, that's `annual expenses / 0.04`, which is the same as multiplying expenses by 25.

**Step 2 — Your coast number today.** Discount the FIRE number back to the present using your expected **real (inflation-adjusted) return**:

```
Coast number = FIRE number / (1 + real return) ^ years to retirement
```

Using a **real return** keeps everything in today's dollars, so your FIRE number doesn't need an inflation adjustment of its own. A common assumption is a ~5% real return for a stock-heavy portfolio (roughly 7–8% nominal minus ~2–3% inflation), but you should test your own.

If your current invested balance is at or above the coast number, congratulations — you're coasting. If not, the gap tells you how much more you need before you can ease off contributions.

## A worked example

Meet Maya, age **30**. She wants **$40,000 a year** in retirement and plans to retire at **60** — that's **30 years** of growth ahead.

- **FIRE number:** $40,000 / 0.04 = **$1,000,000**
- **Coast number today:** $1,000,000 / (1.05)^30 ≈ **$231,000**

So if Maya has about **$231,000** invested today and earns a 5% real return, she can stop contributing entirely. By age 60 that balance grows to roughly $1,000,000 in today's dollars — all on its own. She still needs a job to pay rent and groceries, but not to fund retirement.

If she *doesn't* have $231,000 yet, the number still helps: it's a concrete, motivating finish line for the saving phase.

## Coast number at different ages and returns

Your coast number drops sharply the younger you are, because compounding has more time to work. Here's the coast number for the same **$1,000,000** FIRE goal, retiring at 60, across different starting ages and real returns:

| Current age | Years to 60 | At 4% real | At 5% real | At 6% real |
|------------|------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 25 | 35 | $253,000 | $181,000 | $130,000 |
| 30 | 30 | $308,000 | $231,000 | $174,000 |
| 35 | 25 | $375,000 | $295,000 | $233,000 |
| 40 | 20 | $456,000 | $377,000 | $312,000 |
| 45 | 15 | $555,000 | $481,000 | $417,000 |

Two lessons jump out. Starting early is enormously powerful — a 25-year-old needs barely half what a 45-year-old does for the same goal. And your return assumption matters a lot: the difference between 4% and 6% nearly doubles the required balance at younger ages.

## Why Coast FIRE is worth aiming for

Coast FIRE buys you **options**, and that's the real payoff:

- **Career flexibility.** You can switch to lower-paying but more meaningful work, go part-time, freelance, or take a sabbatical — because the high-earning, high-saving years are behind you.
- **Reduced pressure.** Knowing retirement is handled removes a constant background anxiety. Every paycheck now only has to cover *this year's* life.
- **An achievable milestone.** Full FIRE can feel impossibly distant. Coast FIRE is often reached in your 30s, which makes the whole project feel real.

## Caveats to take seriously

Coast FIRE rests on assumptions, and assumptions can break:

- **Returns may not materialize.** A 5% real return is a long-run *average*, not a guarantee. A weak decade early on changes the picture.
- **Your expense estimate could be off.** A $40,000 lifestyle today may not match the life you want at 60. Revisit the number periodically.
- **Sequence-of-returns risk.** A market crash right as you stop contributing — or right as you retire — hurts more than the same crash mid-journey.
- **Healthcare and the unexpected.** Coast FIRE assumes your job covers current costs *including* health insurance. Losing that coverage, or a major one-off expense, can force you to start saving again.
- **It's not a one-time check.** Treat your coast number as a living target. Re-run it every year or two as your balance, goals, and assumptions evolve.
