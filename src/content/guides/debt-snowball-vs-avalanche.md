If you're juggling multiple debts, the order you pay them off in changes how much interest you pay and how long it takes to be free. Two methods dominate the conversation: the **snowball** and the **avalanche**. Here's how each works and how to pick.

## The ground rules (both methods)

Whatever you choose, the foundation is the same:

1. Always pay the **minimum** on every debt so nothing goes into default.
2. Find every extra dollar you can put toward debt each month.
3. Send that extra money to **one** target debt at a time.
4. When a debt is paid off, roll its old payment onto the next target — this is the "snowball" effect that accelerates everything.

The only thing that differs is **which debt you target first**.

## The avalanche method

Target the debt with the **highest interest rate** first, regardless of balance.

- **Pro:** Mathematically optimal. You always pay the least total interest and usually become debt-free fastest.
- **Con:** If your highest-rate debt also has a big balance, it can take a while before you celebrate your first payoff — which is hard for some people to stick with.

## The snowball method

Target the **smallest balance** first, regardless of interest rate.

- **Pro:** Quick wins. Knocking out a whole debt early is motivating and frees up that minimum payment fast.
- **Con:** You may pay slightly more interest overall, because a high-rate debt could sit untouched while you clear smaller ones.

## A worked example

Say you have three debts and $800/month to spend:

| Debt | Balance | APR | Minimum |
|------|--------:|----:|--------:|
| Credit card | $6,000 | 22.9% | $150 |
| Car loan | $12,000 | 7.5% | $280 |
| Personal loan | $4,000 | 13% | $110 |

- **Avalanche** attacks the 22.9% credit card first — starving the most expensive debt — and typically saves the most interest.
- **Snowball** attacks the $4,000 personal loan first, giving you a completed payoff sooner.

The exact dollar gap between them depends on your numbers, which is why it's worth running both. Use the [debt payoff calculator](/calculators/debt-payoff) — it shows the timeline and total interest for each method side by side.

## So which should you choose?

Here's the honest answer: **the best method is the one you'll actually finish.**

- Choose **avalanche** if you're disciplined and want to save every possible dollar.
- Choose **snowball** if past attempts fizzled out and you need momentum to stay in the game.

The difference in total interest is usually modest. The difference between *finishing* and *quitting* is everything.

## The lever both methods ignore: your interest rate

Neither method lowers your APR. If your cards are above ~15%, look into a **0% balance-transfer card** or a **fixed-rate consolidation loan**. Cutting the rate can shorten your payoff more than any change in strategy — just watch for transfer fees and make sure you can clear the balance before any promotional rate ends.
