An emergency fund is the foundation everything else is built on. Without one, a single car repair or medical bill becomes credit-card debt — and that debt undoes years of progress. Here's how to size and build yours.

## How much do you need?

The standard guidance is **three to six months of essential expenses**. Where you land depends on your situation:

- **Closer to 3 months** if you have very stable income, dual earners, or few dependents.
- **Closer to 6+ months** if you're self-employed, a single income, in a volatile industry, or supporting a family.

Note the word *essential*. Your emergency fund covers needs — rent, food, utilities, insurance, minimum debt payments — not your full lifestyle. Use the needs figure from the [budget calculator](/calculators/budget) as your monthly base, then multiply.

## Where to keep it

An emergency fund has two jobs: be **safe** and be **available fast**. That rules out the stock market (it can crash exactly when you need the cash) and anything with withdrawal penalties.

The best home is a **high-yield savings account**: FDIC-insured, accessible within a day or two, and currently paying around 4–5% — many times what a traditional big-bank account offers. Keep it in a *separate* account from your checking so you're not tempted to dip in.

## How to build it without stalling everything else

You don't have to choose between an emergency fund and your other goals — sequence them:

1. **Start with a $1,000 starter fund.** This handles the most common small emergencies and stops new debt immediately.
2. **Pay off high-interest debt** (anything above ~8–10%) next — the guaranteed return from killing that interest beats almost anything else.
3. **Then build to your full 3–6 months** while contributing enough to capture any employer retirement match.

Automate a fixed monthly transfer toward the target. The [savings goal calculator](/calculators/savings-goal) will tell you exactly how much per month to hit your number by a chosen date.

## When (and how) to use it

Use it for genuine emergencies: job loss, urgent medical costs, essential home or car repairs. Not for holidays or sales — those are planned expenses that belong in your budget.

And when you do draw it down, make replenishing it your top priority again. An emergency fund isn't a one-time achievement; it's a buffer you keep topped up for life.
