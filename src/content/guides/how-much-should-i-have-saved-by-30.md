The most widely cited answer comes from Fidelity, which translates "am I on track?" into a simple multiple of your salary. The short version: aim for roughly **1x your salary saved by 30, 3x by 40, and 6x by 50**.

Here are the full benchmarks:

| Age | Target savings |
|-----|----------------|
| 30  | ~1x your salary |
| 40  | ~3x your salary |
| 50  | ~6x your salary |
| 60  | ~8x your salary |
| 67  | ~10x your salary |

That's the headline. The rest of this guide explains what those numbers actually mean, how to use them, and what to do if your balance looks nothing like them.

## These Are Rules of Thumb, Not Rules

Treat the multiples as **guideposts, not grades**. They're built on broad averages and a set of assumptions baked into them, including a retirement age around 67, a roughly 15% savings rate, and a relatively steady investment return over decades.

Your real target depends on factors the table can't see:

- **Lifestyle.** Someone who plans to travel constantly needs more than someone who plans a quiet, low-cost retirement.
- **Retirement age.** Want to stop at 55? You'll need a bigger multiple sooner. Happy to work to 70? You can be lighter at 50.
- **Location.** Retiring in a high-cost city is a different math problem than retiring in a low-cost-of-living town or abroad.
- **Pensions and other income.** A pension or rental income lowers how much you personally need to have saved.

So if you're at 0.8x by 30 instead of a clean 1x, don't panic. The multiples are there to tell you the *direction*, not to fail you by a rounding error.

## A Worked Example

Say you earn **$60,000 a year**. Here's what the benchmarks suggest:

- **By 30:** ~$60,000 saved (1x)
- **By 40:** ~$180,000 saved (3x)
- **By 50:** ~$360,000 saved (6x)
- **By 60:** ~$480,000 saved (8x)

Notice how the jumps get bigger over time even though the *multiple* only steps up by a few points. That's not extra effort on your part. It's compounding doing the heavy lifting, which is exactly why the next section matters.

## What Counts Toward These Numbers

When you measure yourself against the multiples, count the money that's actually working toward retirement:

- **Retirement accounts** — your 401(k), 403(b), IRA, or similar.
- **Employer match** — money your company contributes counts too.
- **Taxable investments** earmarked for the long term — brokerage accounts, index funds, and the like.

What generally *doesn't* count: your emergency fund, your checking account, the equity in the home you live in, or money you've set aside for near-term goals like a car or a wedding. Those are important, but they're not retirement savings.

## Why Starting Early Beats Saving More Later

The single biggest advantage in retirement saving isn't your salary. It's **time**, because of compounding: your returns start earning their own returns, and the effect snowballs the longer money stays invested.

A dollar invested in your 20s has decades to multiply. A dollar invested in your 50s has only a few years. This is why someone who starts modestly at 25 can end up ahead of someone who starts aggressively at 40.

You can see this for yourself with the [compound interest calculator](/calculators/compound-interest). Plug in a small monthly amount over 35 years versus a larger amount over 15, and the gap is usually eye-opening.

## Don't Forget Your Savings Rate

The multiples tell you where you should *be*. Your **savings rate** tells you how to *get there*. A common target is putting around **15% of your income** toward retirement each year, ideally including any employer match.

Think of the two together:

- The **multiple** is your scoreboard ("am I on track for my age?").
- The **rate** is your game plan ("am I putting in enough to stay on track?").

If you're hitting 15% consistently from your 20s or early 30s, the multiples tend to take care of themselves.

## What to Do If You're Behind

Most people are behind at some point, and it is very recoverable. In order of impact:

### 1. Capture your full employer match

If your employer matches contributions and you're not getting the full amount, that's the first fix. It's an immediate, guaranteed return on your money. Contribute at least enough to capture every dollar offered.

### 2. Raise your savings rate gradually

You don't have to jump to 15% overnight. **Increase your contribution by one or two percentage points** each year, and bump it whenever you get a raise so you never feel the cut. Small, automatic increases add up fast.

### 3. Work slightly longer

Even one or two extra years of work makes a large difference. You add contributions, you give your existing balance more time to grow, and you shorten the number of years your savings has to cover. It's one of the most powerful catch-up levers there is.

### 4. Trim lifestyle creep, not your life

Often the easiest dollars to redirect are the ones that quietly crept into your budget as your income rose. Redirecting even part of that toward retirement closes the gap without major sacrifice.

## Put Real Numbers to It

The salary multiples are a fast gut check, but your situation is specific. The best next step is to run your own numbers: your current balance, your contribution rate, your target retirement age, and the lifestyle you want.
