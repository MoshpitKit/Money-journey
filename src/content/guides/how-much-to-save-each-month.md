There's no single dollar amount that's right for everyone, but there are solid anchors you can use to set a realistic monthly savings target. The honest answer depends on your income, your fixed costs, and what you're saving *for*. Below are three reliable ways to land on a number, the order to fund your goals in, and how to make progress even when money is tight.

## Start With Three Useful Anchors

Most people do best by combining a percentage-based baseline with goal-based math. Here are the three benchmarks worth knowing.

### 1. 20% of take-home pay (the baseline)

This comes from the popular **50/30/20 budget**: 50% of after-tax income for needs, 30% for wants, and **20% for savings and debt paydown**. It's a clean starting point because it scales with what you actually bring home.

If 20% feels out of reach right now, that's normal — treat it as a target to grow into, not a pass/fail test. For the full framework, see [The 50/30/20 Rule Explained](/guides/50-30-20-rule-explained).

### 2. ~15% of gross income for retirement specifically

A widely used rule of thumb for retirement is to put away about **15% of your gross (pre-tax) income** each year, including any employer 401(k) match. This is separate from the 20% baseline above — the 20% covers *all* savings (emergency fund, short-term goals, retirement), while the 15% is a long-term retirement-only guideline. As your retirement contributions ramp up, they become a big chunk of your overall savings rate.

### 3. Goal-based saving (work backwards)

Percentages are a starting point, but your real number often comes from **working backwards from specific goals**. The formula is simple:

> Monthly savings = (Target amount − what you've already saved) ÷ months until the deadline

For example, a $9,000 emergency fund you want fully funded in 18 months means about **$500/month**. A $6,000 down payment on a car in two years is **$250/month**. Add your goals together and you get a personalized target. A [savings goal calculator](/calculators/savings-goal) does this math for you across multiple goals at once.

## The Priority Order: What to Fund First

When you can't do everything at once, sequence matters. This order minimizes risk and avoids leaving free money or paying needless interest:

1. **Starter emergency fund** — Save a small cushion (often $1,000, or one month of essential expenses) so a surprise bill doesn't go on a credit card.
2. **Capture the full 401(k) match** — If your employer matches contributions, contribute at least enough to get the entire match. It's an immediate, guaranteed return you won't find anywhere else.
3. **Pay off high-interest debt** — Attack balances like credit cards (often 20%+ APR). No investment reliably beats that rate, so clearing it *is* a high return.
4. **Full emergency fund** — Build to **3–6 months of essential expenses** (lean toward 6+ if your income is variable or your job is less stable).
5. **Retirement to ~15%** — Increase contributions toward the 15%-of-gross target across your 401(k), IRA, or both.
6. **Other goals** — Down payment, a car, a wedding, kids' education, or simply investing more. This is where goal-based math takes over.

If you're ready for step 5 or 6 and want to start small, [How to Start Investing With Little Money](/guides/how-to-start-investing) walks through low-cost ways to begin.

## Saving by Income Level (Conceptually)

The right percentage shifts with where you are:

- **Tighter budgets:** When a large share of income goes to non-negotiable costs like rent and groceries, hitting 20% may not be realistic yet. Focus on saving *something* consistently and protecting the 401(k) match.
- **Middle incomes:** This is where the 20% baseline tends to fit well. Automating it before lifestyle creep sets in is the biggest lever.
- **Higher incomes:** Because fixed needs take up a smaller slice, many people can save well above 20% — and often should, especially to make up for late starts or to reach goals like early retirement.

The pattern: as income rises, your *savings rate* (not just the dollar amount) can usually climb too.

## How to Save When Money Is Tight

A small, steady habit beats an ambitious plan you abandon. If 20% is impossible today:

- **Save something — anything.** Even **$25–$50 a month** builds the habit and gives you a cushion. Momentum matters more than the starting amount.
- **Automate it.** Set up an automatic transfer to savings on payday so the money moves before you can spend it. What you don't see, you don't miss.
- **Increase with every raise.** When your pay goes up, send half (or more) of the increase straight to savings. You never adjust to spending money you never started spending.
- **Funnel windfalls.** Tax refunds, bonuses, and cash gifts are a chance to jump ahead on a goal without touching your regular budget.
- **Revisit quarterly.** Bump your savings rate by even 1% every few months. The increases are small enough to barely notice but add up fast over a year.

## The Bottom Line

Start with **20% of take-home** as a baseline, aim for **~15% of gross toward retirement** over time, and let **specific goals** set your exact monthly number. Follow the priority order so you never leave a match on the table or pay avoidable interest — and if 20% isn't doable yet, save what you can and raise it as your income grows.
