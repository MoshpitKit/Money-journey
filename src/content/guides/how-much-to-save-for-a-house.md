If you've ever Googled *how much should I save for a house*, you've probably run into the same intimidating answer: **20% down**. On a typical home, that's tens of thousands of dollars, and it stops a lot of would-be buyers before they start.

Here's the good news: **20% down is not a requirement.** It's a benchmark that helps you avoid one specific cost. Once you understand what that cost is, you can decide whether to wait and save more, or buy sooner with less.

## The 20% Down Myth

The "20%" rule exists because of **private mortgage insurance (PMI)**. When you put down less than 20% on a conventional loan, the lender adds PMI to protect itself in case you default. But you can absolutely buy with far less:

- **Conventional loans:** as little as **3% down** for many qualified buyers.
- **FHA loans:** **3.5% down** with a credit score around 580 or higher.
- **VA and USDA loans:** **0% down** for eligible military borrowers and certain rural buyers.

So the real question isn't "Can I buy with less than 20%?" You usually can. The question is whether a bigger or smaller down payment fits your situation.

### Bigger vs. Smaller Down Payment

A **larger down payment** means:

- No PMI (at 20%+), or PMI that drops off faster.
- A smaller loan, so **lower monthly payments** and less interest over the life of the loan.
- A stronger offer in a competitive market.

A **smaller down payment** means:

- You buy sooner and stop paying rent.
- You keep more cash for emergencies and home repairs.
- You pay PMI temporarily (often **~0.3%–1.5% of the loan per year**, varying by credit and down payment), which usually cancels once you reach ~20% equity.

Neither is "right." Buying sooner with PMI can beat waiting years to save 20%, especially if home prices and rent keep rising. Use the [mortgage calculator](/calculators/mortgage) to compare the monthly payment at different down payment levels.

## The Number Nobody Mentions: Total Cash to Close

The down payment is only one piece. To buy a house, you actually need to save for **four** things:

1. **Down payment** — your percentage of the purchase price.
2. **Closing costs** — typically **~2%–5% of the price**, covering loan origination, appraisal, title, taxes, and prepaid insurance.
3. **Cash reserves** — money left over *after* closing. Aim to keep a **3–6 month emergency fund** intact, plus a cushion for immediate repairs.
4. **Moving and furnishing** — movers, deposits, a fridge, a lawnmower, the stuff that adds up fast.

Many first-time buyers budget the down payment perfectly and forget the other three. Then they close with an empty bank account, one broken water heater away from a credit-card spiral.

## Worked Example: A $350,000 Home

Here's how the cash target changes with your down payment on a **$350,000** home. Closing costs below are estimated at **3% of the price (~$10,500)** as a midpoint.

| Down payment | Down payment amount | Est. closing costs | Cash to close |
|---|---|---|---|
| **3%** | $10,500 | $10,500 | **$21,000** |
| **10%** | $35,000 | $10,500 | **$45,500** |
| **20%** | $70,000 | $10,500 | **$80,500** |

Now add reserves and moving costs on top. If you want to keep a **$15,000 emergency fund** and budget **$5,000** for moving and furnishing, your real savings goals look like this:

- **3% down:** ~$21,000 to close **+ $20,000 cushion = ~$41,000 total**
- **10% down:** ~$45,500 to close **+ $20,000 cushion = ~$65,500 total**
- **20% down:** ~$80,500 to close **+ $20,000 cushion = ~$100,500 total**

Seeing the full picture changes the math. The gap between 3% and 20% here isn't $59,500, it's the difference between starting in a year or starting in five. (Note: actual closing costs vary by state and lender, and a seller may agree to cover some of them.)

Not sure what price range to target in the first place? Start with [How Much House Can You Afford?](/guides/how-much-house-can-you-afford) and work backward from a payment you're comfortable with.

## Where to Keep Your Down Payment Savings

This is money you'll need within a few years, so the rule is simple: **do not put it in the stock market.** A 20% market drop the month before you buy could wipe out years of saving.

Instead, keep house savings somewhere **safe and liquid**:

- **High-yield savings account (HYSA):** FDIC-insured, easy access, competitive interest. The best fit for most buyers.
- **Money-market account or fund:** similar safety and yield, often with check-writing.
- **Short-term CDs or T-bills:** good if your timeline is fixed and you won't need the cash early.

The goal isn't to *grow* this money aggressively. It's to **protect it** while earning a little on the side. Keep it separate from your everyday checking so you're not tempted to dip in.

## Turning the Target Into a Monthly Number

A big lump sum feels impossible. A monthly number feels like a plan. The formula is straightforward:

> **Total goal ÷ months until you want to buy = monthly savings target**

Say you're aiming for **$45,500** (10% down on that $350,000 home) in **three years**:

- $45,500 ÷ 36 months = **about $1,265 per month**

If that's too steep, you have levers to pull: extend your timeline, lower your target price, choose a smaller down payment, or factor in the interest your HYSA earns along the way. Don't forget **down payment assistance programs** — many states and cities offer grants or low-interest second loans for first-time and moderate-income buyers.

## The Bottom Line

How much should you save for a house? **Enough to cover your chosen down payment, plus closing costs, plus a cushion you don't touch.** For a $350,000 home that's roughly **$41,000 at 3% down** or **$100,000+ at 20% down**, and the smaller number is a perfectly legitimate way to become a homeowner.

Pick a realistic target, park it in a high-yield account, and automate a monthly transfer. The sooner you set the number, the sooner you hit it.
