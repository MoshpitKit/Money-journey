Compound interest is the single most important concept in personal finance, and also one of the most misunderstood. Here's what it actually is, why it matters so much, and how to put it to work.

## Simple vs compound interest

**Simple interest** is calculated only on your original amount. Put $1,000 in an account paying 5% simple interest and you earn $50 every year — forever. After 30 years you'd have $2,500.

**Compound interest** is calculated on your original amount *plus all the interest you've already earned*. That same $1,000 at 5% compounded annually becomes about $4,322 after 30 years — nearly double the simple-interest result, from the exact same deposit and rate.

The difference is that your interest starts earning interest. That's the whole game.

## Why time matters more than money

Because compounding builds on itself, the years at the start of your journey are worth far more than the years at the end. Consider two savers, both earning 7% a year:

- **Alex** invests $300/month from age 25 to 35 — just ten years, $36,000 total — then stops and never adds another dollar.
- **Sam** waits until 35, then invests $300/month all the way to 65 — thirty years, $108,000 total.

At 65, Alex (who invested a third as much) ends up with *more money than Sam*. Alex's early contributions had an extra decade to compound, and that head start is nearly impossible to catch.

The lesson: the best time to start was years ago. The second-best time is today.

## The Rule of 72

Want a quick way to estimate growth without a calculator? Divide 72 by your interest rate to find roughly how many years it takes your money to double.

- At 4%: 72 ÷ 4 = **18 years** to double
- At 7%: 72 ÷ 7 ≈ **10 years** to double
- At 10%: 72 ÷ 10 ≈ **7 years** to double

This is why even a couple of extra percentage points of return matters enormously over a lifetime.

## How to actually earn it

Compounding only helps if your money is somewhere that pays a real return:

1. **Cash you'll need soon** — a high-yield savings account (currently around 4–5%) keeps it safe and liquid.
2. **Long-term money** — low-cost, diversified index funds inside a tax-advantaged account (401(k), IRA) have historically delivered around 7% a year after inflation, though with ups and downs.
3. **Automate contributions** — set a recurring transfer so compounding never waits on your willpower.
