"How much do I need to retire?" has a surprisingly simple starting answer — and then a lot of useful nuance. Let's walk through both.

## Start with your spending, not your salary

Your retirement number depends on how much you'll *spend*, not how much you earn now. The classic estimate:

> **Retirement target = annual spending ÷ 4%**

This comes from the **4% rule** — research suggesting you can withdraw about 4% of a diversified portfolio in your first year of retirement, adjust for inflation each year after, and have a strong chance the money lasts 30+ years.

So if you expect to spend **$60,000 a year**, your target is roughly:

$60,000 ÷ 0.04 = **$1.5 million**

Want spending of $40,000? You need about $1 million. $80,000? About $2 million. The math scales linearly, which makes it easy to estimate.

## Don't forget inflation

$60,000 today won't buy $60,000 of stuff in 30 years. At ~2.5% inflation, you'd need roughly **$126,000** of future income to match $60,000 of today's lifestyle three decades out — which raises the nest-egg target accordingly. ## Age-based benchmarks (a quick gut check)

Fidelity's widely cited guideline for how much to have saved, as a multiple of your salary:

| Age | Saved (× salary) |
|----:|-----------------:|
| 30 | 1× |
| 40 | 3× |
| 50 | 6× |
| 60 | 8× |
| 67 | 10× |

These are rough averages, not rules — but if you're well below the line for your age, it's a signal to increase contributions.

## The levers you control

If your projection shows a shortfall, you have five moves:

1. **Save more each month** — the most direct lever.
2. **Start earlier** — compounding rewards time more than amount.
3. **Work a bit longer** — this adds savings *and* shortens the drawdown, a double win.
4. **Spend less in retirement** — lowering the target shrinks the number fast.
5. **Use tax-advantaged accounts** — 401(k) and IRA growth isn't taxed yearly, so it compounds faster. Capture any employer match first; it's free money.

## A caveat on the 4% rule

The 4% rule is a planning anchor, not a guarantee. Real retirements face market crashes early on ("sequence of returns risk"), rising healthcare costs, and unknown lifespans. Many planners now treat 3.5%–4% as a sensible range and stay flexible with spending. Use your number as a target to aim for, then revisit the plan every year or two.
